# The Glass Wall in Global Tech Pay  
### Remote work went global. Compensation didn’t.

*Last updated: 5 April 2026 (Australia/Melbourne)*

---

## Why I wrote this

There’s a moment a lot of international remote workers eventually experience—especially when you’re good enough to compete for top-tier roles, but you live outside the United States.

It’s not a dramatic moment. No villain monologue. No email that says “we pay you less because you’re overseas.”

It’s subtler than that.

It’s the moment you realize you’re not in a single global market at all.

You’re in a **segmented market**—a world where the same job can be priced in two different realities depending on where your body sleeps at night. And the machinery that enforces that segmentation often looks completely reasonable on paper: compliance, payroll, tax, “local norms,” “cost of labor.”

But the lived outcome is hard to defend:

> **International talent is allowed to do the work, but often not allowed to set the price.**

This post investigates *how* that happens, why it’s “legitimate” enough to be normal, and why it’s still unfair. It ends with a concrete proposal for paying more egalitarianly.

---

## 1) The hiring stack that creates two labor markets

A U.S. company that wants to hire internationally usually picks one of three routes:

1) **Open a local entity** (slow, expensive, operationally heavy)  
2) Use an **Employer of Record (EOR)**  
3) Hire as a **contractor**, sometimes through an agency/platform

EORs matter because they institutionalize a split:

- The U.S. company manages your **day-to-day work**, goals, and performance.  
- The EOR becomes your **legal employer** (contracts, payroll, withholding, compliance, benefits).  

Even when nobody is trying to be exploitative, this structure becomes a kind of **wage membrane**: it makes it easy to treat pay as local by default, even when the work is globally competitive.

---

## 2) Pay localization isn’t a rumor. Companies publish it.

Many companies explicitly say they pay **local market rates**.

Some do this by “cost of labor” (local market benchmarking), not by “cost of living.”

Other remote-first companies move closer to location-agnostic pay, but those are still the exception.

The point isn’t that any one company is “bad.” The point is structural:

> **If remote work is global but pay is local-by-policy, then a single global labor market never actually forms.**

---

## 3) The research: “global marketplaces” still price workers by country

If you want one powerful, research-backed line:

Even on platforms where jobs are remote and competition is global, **wages remain country-priced** to a striking degree, and wage differences correlate strongly with **GDP per capita** in the worker’s country.

In other words, remote work doesn’t automatically dissolve borders in compensation. It often reproduces them.

---

## 4) The double-discount nobody talks about

There are (at least) two downward forces that can stack:

1) **Remote discount** — remote work is a valued amenity; many workers accept less for it.  
2) **Location discount** — pay is then adjusted down again because the worker is outside the U.S.

Even if you dispute the exact sizes, the structure is real: a worker can be “discounted” twice for the same job.

---

## 5) The numbers: A$203k vs US$212k isn’t “close” (FX, PPP, and Super)

You asked for numbers that make this real. Here they are.

### 5.1 Market FX comparison (early April 2026 snapshot)

Using a mid-market AUD/USD snapshot:

- **A$203,000 ≈ US$139,948**
- **US$212,000 ≈ A$307,514**

That means **A$203k is ~66% of US$212k** on pure FX.

### 5.2 Big Mac Index purchasing-power check (Jan 2026)

Big Mac prices (Jan 2026):

- United States: **US$6.12**
- Australia: **A$8.50**

Implied “burger PPP” exchange rate:

- **1 USD ≈ 1.389 AUD**  
- **1 AUD ≈ 0.720 USD**

PPP-ish conversions:

- **A$203,000 ≈ US$146,160 (PPP-ish)**
- **US$212,000 ≈ A$294,444 (PPP-ish)**

Even after this rough purchasing-power adjustment, **A$203k is still only ~69% of US$212k**.

### 5.3 The Australia-specific missing piece: Superannuation

Australian compensation has a trap-door that blows up comparisons: **super**.

In Australia, employers must contribute Superannuation Guarantee (SG) at a statutory rate for eligible workers. For FY 2025–26, that rate is **12%**.

But job offers and salary discussions can be framed in two different ways:

#### A) “Base + super” (exclusive of super)

If **A$203,000 is base salary**, and super is *on top*:

- Employer super on top (12%): **A$24,360**
- Total employer cost (base + SG): **A$227,360**

#### B) “Total package inclusive of super”

If **A$203,000 is total package (inclusive)**, then base salary is smaller:

- Base salary (inside the package): **A$181,250**
- Super component (within): **A$21,750**

**This is why international salary comparisons go wrong in casual conversation.**  
Two Australians can both say “I’m on 203k,” but one might mean base, the other might mean package.

### 5.4 A more honest apples-to-apples: *total employer cost*

If we’re going to be fair, we should compare **what the company pays** (not just the base paycheck). For the U.S., that includes employer payroll taxes and often benefits.

Illustrative U.S. employer payroll taxes for a US$212k employee in 2026:

- Employer Social Security tax: 6.2% up to the wage base (cap applies)  
- Employer Medicare tax: 1.45% (no cap)

That comes to roughly:

- Employer payroll taxes (illustrative): **US$14,513**

And that’s before health insurance, 401(k) match, etc. (Those vary wildly, but they are real employer-side costs in many U.S. offers.)

**Key point:** Australia has “mandatory employer retirement contribution” (super) that people forget to include; the U.S. often has “mandatory employer payroll taxes” (and frequently employer-paid health premiums) that people forget to include.

If we want clean arguments, we need clean comparisons.

## 5.5 Case study: same company, same team — Melbourne vs Phoenix

Let’s take the scenario you described:

- **Person A (Melbourne):** A$203,000  
- **Person B (Phoenix, Arizona):** US$212,000  
- Same company, same team, same problems.

### Step 1 — Convert Person A into USD using a real FX source

The U.S. Federal Reserve’s H.10 series shows AUD was roughly **US$0.70–US$0.715 per A$1** in March 2026.  
Using that range:

- **A$203,000 ≈ US$141,998 to US$145,145**  
- **US$212,000 stays US$212,000**

So Person B earns **~46% to ~49% more** *before* we even talk about costs of living.

> **To equalize *cash salary* at market FX, Person A would need about A$296,503–A$303,074, not A$203,000.**

### Step 2 — Could local cost-of-living plausibly erase that gap?

For the pay gap to disappear on “standard of living” grounds, Phoenix would need to be **~146% to ~149% more expensive** than Melbourne.

Even using a *rough, crowdsourced* city comparison (Numbeo), Phoenix is estimated to be **~5.7% lower** than Melbourne on “cost of living including rent.” If that’s even directionally correct, then the gap gets *worse*, not better:

- After this cost-of-living adjustment, Person B’s effective advantage is roughly **~55% to ~58%**.

### Step 3 — What about mandatory employer contributions (Australia super vs U.S. payroll taxes)?

Australia adds a compulsory employer super contribution: **12%** (FY 2025–26).

If we compare “what the employer pays,” not just salary:

- Person A employer cost if **base + super**:  
  - **A$203,000 + 12% SG = A$227,360**  
  - ≈ **US$159,038 to US$162,562** at the same FX range

- Person B employer payroll taxes (Social Security up to the wage base + Medicare):  
  - ≈ **US$14,513**  
  - Total employer cost ≈ **US$226,513**

Even on employer cost, Person B is still **~39% to ~42% higher**.

### Verdict on “unequivocal imbalance”

Yes — this is a clean, robust demonstration of a regional pay imbalance:

- It’s not sensitive to the exact exchange rate (within plausible ranges).  
- It’s not plausibly explained by Melbourne vs Phoenix cost-of-living differences.  
- Even adding mandatory employer-side contributions (super / payroll taxes) doesn’t erase it.

It’s exactly what a segmented labor market looks like: **same work, different price, based on where you live.**


## 6) Why this is unfair (even when it’s “policy” and “compliance”)

Let’s grant the strongest employer arguments:

- Cross-border employment is legally complex.  
- EORs reduce risk and speed up hiring.  
- Companies benchmark pay using local labor markets.

None of that erases the fairness problem.

### Fairness problem #1: location is not a clean choice
People are anchored by partners, kids, visas, caregiving, health, aging parents, citizenship constraints. Paying less for location often penalizes constraints.

### Fairness problem #2: localization prices bargaining power, not value
When pay is anchored to “what your local market tolerates,” the system prices your leverage, not the competitive value of your work.

### Fairness problem #3: it reproduces global inequality by design
If remote wages are still heavily explained by country and GDP per capita, then “remote work” becomes a *new channel* for old inequality rather than a correction.

### Fairness problem #4: intermediaries normalize the segmentation
EORs and local contracting structures make it feel “natural” that pay is local, even when the work is globally priced.

## 7) Proposal: the Egalitarian Pay Compact (a realistic path to fairer pay)

This is a proposal designed to survive first contact with compliance, finance, and executive skepticism.

### Principle 1 — Price the role globally, not the worker locally
Create global bands by role/level anchored to an explicit benchmark.

### Principle 2 — Separate “mandatory employer costs” from “salary”
Publish offers as:

- **Base pay**
- **Mandatory employer contributions** (e.g., Australia SG; U.S. employer payroll taxes)
- **Optional benefits value** (e.g., insurance premiums, retirement match)

This instantly reduces confusion and removes rhetorical games.

### Principle 3 — If you use location factors, cap them tightly
If leadership refuses parity, impose a narrow corridor (example: 0.90–1.05), and require written justification outside it.

### Principle 4 — Add a purchasing-power floor
Even if you don’t go full parity, set a rule:

> no employee falls below **X% of PPP parity** relative to the reference band.

### Principle 5 — Radical transparency
Publish:
- global bands
- location factor (if any)
- how EOR/contractor roles map into bands
- and how mandatory employer costs are treated

Transparency isn’t just ethics—it’s future-proofing as norms and laws trend toward pay disclosure.

### Principle 6 — A phased plan to parity
If “we can’t afford it now,” then make it a timeline, not a permanent excuse.

---

## 8) What I’m asking companies to do

- **Stop using geography to discount talent.**  
- **Pay for the role at a global rate.**  
- **Treat jurisdiction complexity as a company cost, not a worker penalty.**  
- **If you must use location factors, cap them and publish them.**  
- **Commit to a timeline toward parity.**

## Addendum: factoring in 15,000 stock options (strike $2.30, current $11.50)

**Assumption:** These are standard options (not RSUs), fully vested and exercisable today.

- Options: 15,000
- Strike: $2.30
- Current: $11.50
- Intrinsic value per option: $9.20
- **Total intrinsic value (pre-tax): $138,000**

### Salary-only comparison (USD)

- Person A salary: **US$141,998 to US$145,145**
- Person B salary: **US$212,000**
- Gap: **US$66,855 to US$70,002** (**~46% to ~49%**)

### Total compensation including options (intrinsic value)

- Person A total: **US$279,998 to US$283,145**
- Person B total: **US$350,000**
- Gap: **US$66,855 to US$70,002** (**~24% to ~25%**)

### Employer-cost lens (Australia super on top; US employer payroll taxes) + options

- Person A employer cost + options: **US$297,038 to US$300,562**
- Person B employer cost + options: **US$364,513**
- Gap: **US$63,951 to US$67,475** (**~21% to ~23%**)

### Interpretation

Because both people receive the **same equity grant**, adding options:

- **does not change the absolute gap much** (it stays roughly a ~$67k–$70k difference in the salary-only view), but  
- it **reduces the *percentage* gap** because the denominator (total comp) is larger.

If these were **RSUs** instead of options, the equity value would be **$172,500** (15,000 × $11.50), and the percentage gap would shrink further (to roughly **~21%–~22%**), while the absolute gap stays similar.

**Bottom line:** the conclusion is the same—there is still a real regional pay imbalance—but it looks *less extreme* if equity is a huge and equal component of compensation.

> Practical note: with options, this “value” is only real if you can actually exercise and sell (public liquidity) or if there’s a liquidation event (private company), and it’s also affected by vesting schedules and tax treatment.
